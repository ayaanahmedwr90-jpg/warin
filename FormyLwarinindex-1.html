Period Help – when your body and heart are both tired

Important: I am not a doctor. These are just gentle, general ideas. If your pain or bleeding is unusual, very strong, or if you feel that something is really wrong, please contact a real doctor, nurse, clinic, or emergency service.
  • If you have one, you can use a warm hot‑water bottle or heating pad on your lower belly or lower back (not too hot – protect your skin with a cloth or towel).
  • Sipping warm water or a gentle warm drink can feel comforting for many people.
  • If a doctor has already recommended a painkiller (like ibuprofen or paracetamol), you can follow that advice. Don’t start new medicines on your own – ask a doctor or pharmacist first.
  • For some people, very light movement (slow walking around the room, gentle stretching) can reduce cramps a little. But if it hurts, stop. You don’t have to push your body.
  • If you notice that every month the pain is getting worse, or it stops you from doing normal daily things completely, it’s a good idea to talk to a doctor or gynecologist when you can.

Because of hormones, feelings can become very intense during your period. You are not doing this “on purpose” – it’s not because you are a bad person.

  • Try a few slow deep breaths (you can also use the “Breathe with Stars” game above).
  • Make or open a playlist with songs that feel comforting – not necessarily happy, just songs that understand your mood.
  • Use something soft and grounding – for example, hugging a pillow, a soft toy, or wrapping yourself in a blanket.
  • If words feel too hard, you can still message: “I’m not okay today, but I don’t know what to say.” That is already enough information for someone who loves you.
  • If you feel like talking, you can tell me honestly how you feel. If you don’t want to talk, that’s also okay – I’m learning to respect your quiet too.

Even when you are angry or want to push me away, the fact that you are still here matters to me. And if you need space, I will try to give it without making you feel guilty.

  • Headaches can get worse when you are dehydrated, so small sips of water throughout the day can help.
  • Give your eyes a little rest from bright screens if you can. Even 5–10 minutes with your eyes closed helps.
  • Very gentle self‑massage on temples, neck, or shoulders may relax some tension, if it feels okay to you.
  • A warm shower or bath (if available and safe) can relax muscles and help your body feel softer.
  • If the pain is much stronger than usual, or you feel dizzy, sick, or very weak, please don’t wait – contact a doctor or health professional.

Often helpful for many people:

  • Warm water or low‑caffeine warm drinks (only if your body is okay with them).
  • Fruits like banana, orange, or others you like and can digest well.
  • Light meals – rice with lentils, soup, soft and simple foods.
  • A little dark chocolate can make some people feel better (if it suits your body).
  • Foods with iron and vitamins (like leafy greens, beans, eggs, etc.), if your body can handle them and you like them.

Things that can make some people feel worse (not everyone):

  • Very spicy or very oily food, which can upset your stomach.
  • Lots of cold sugary drinks or soft drinks.
  • Too much coffee or strong tea (high caffeine) – can increase anxiety and make it harder to sleep.

Your body is unique. If you notice that a certain food always makes your cramps or mood worse, you can try to avoid it during your period. And if you are unsure, asking a doctor or nutrition professional is always safest.

If you opened this, maybe you feel overwhelmed, hurt, and like everything – including me – is too much right now.

If one day you truly decide you want to leave, I won’t try to control your choice. But before that, I want you to know a few things:

  • You are not a burden to me. Your pain is real, and I don’t want to minimise it.
  • During period days, feelings often become more extreme – wanting to run away, to block everyone, to disappear. A few days later, the intensity can slowly go down again.
  • You are allowed to rest, cry, be silent, or be distant for a while. You don’t have to pretend to be okay for me.

I’m not saying this to hold you by force, but to remind you: you are more lovable than you believe, even on the days you can’t love yourself.

Everyone’s period is different, but there are some warning signs where getting medical help is really important. Please consider talking to a doctor or going to a clinic if you notice:

  • Sudden, very strong pain that is much worse than your normal period pain.
  • Bleeding so heavy that you are soaking through a pad or tampon every hour for several hours.
  • Large blood clots (for example, bigger than a coin) happening again and again.
  • Strong pain together with fever, feeling very sick, or a strange smell from vaginal discharge.
  • Your period suddenly stops for a long time and there is a chance you might be pregnant.
  • Periods that are irregular in a new way (for example, suddenly much more painful or much heavier than before).
  • Feeling so dizzy, weak, or breathless that it’s hard to stand or walk normally.

If anything scares you or feels very different from your usual experience, it’s always okay (and smart) to ask a doctor or nurse. You deserve real medical care when you need it.

Period days can make existing sadness, anxiety, or old wounds feel louder. Taking care of your mind is just as important as taking care of your body.

  • If you notice thoughts like “I don’t want to exist” or “Everyone is better without me,” that’s a signal that you deserve support – not that you are weak.
  • When those thoughts come, try to slow everything down: breathe, drink a sip of water, maybe message or call someone safe (including me).
  • If you ever feel like you might seriously hurt yourself, please contact local emergency services, a crisis line, or a trusted person nearby. Your safety is more important than anything else.
  • You can write your feelings in a note (phone or paper) without having to make them sound “pretty”. Just getting them out of your head can help a little.
  • Remember: hormones can make dark thoughts louder, but they are not the full truth about who you are or how loved you are.

If your mental health feels heavy very often (not only during your period), talking with a therapist or counsellor in your country could help a lot. There is nothing shameful about needing that.

Sometimes it helps to track what happens each month, so you can see patterns and explain things better to yourself or a doctor. You don’t have to be perfect with this.

  • In a notes app or small notebook, you can write:
    • Day 1 of bleeding,
    • How strong the pain is (for example 1–10),
    • Big mood changes (sad / angry / calm / anxious),
    • Anything special (like headaches, nausea, very heavy bleeding, etc.).
  • After a few months, you might see a pattern – like which days are usually the hardest for you.
  • Knowing your pattern can help you plan softer days for yourself, and it can also help a doctor understand what’s going on if you ever need to ask for help.

You don’t owe anyone this data – it’s just for you, so that you feel a little more in control of your own body story.
